Evan Olszewski - On More's Utopia

            More Than Coincidence: State-Sponsored Religion Supports State Ends

 

The Utopian state’s idea and implementation of religion is the critical element of the society’s cohesion. Though More mentions this topic last of all it is the most important, as the principles relegated to the priest class’ care form the foundation of the rational selection and synthesis of ideas of which the Utopians are most proud. Utopian society is perfectly designed to accommodate communal living, but the author depends upon the social force of the clergy to unite and motivate the people toward maintaining the ideal society. Since this ideal society is based so heavily on moral principles, it is a wonder that the Utopians are so tolerant of competing perspectives. However, the beauty of More’s possession-less society is that ideas are considered communal just like material goods, so citizens are free to think about, debate, and adopt whichever principles best serve society’s ends. More’s religion is unique because it contributes to Utopia’s success by proscribing political principles and allowing morals to be derived from them.

More constructs religion as an auxiliary to the Utopian government. Just as the Stywards are tasked with meeting the organizational needs of the people, the priests are tasked with treating the citizens’ moral needs. In his comparison of Utopia to contemporary societies, Raphael says that the priests and aristocratic classes don’t contribute anything meaningful to the public and are therefore unnecessary (Utopia, 57). This suggests that More believes Utopian society could exist perfectly well without any sort of religious procedures. However, in the next breath of discussion, Raphael elaborates on the dining situation in Utopia, which includes preferential seating for the well-respected priests (63), and later elaborates on the Utopians’ religious observances and beliefs. Because of this, it seems the author is actually recommending a religious system that contributes something useful to the public. As such, the priests of a community are elected by the people and bear the responsibilities of conscience just as the Stywards can be said to bear the responsibilities of legislation. It is the priests’ job to provide moral education to the young (105), and the text leaves us with the understanding that these moral principles are in exact concordance with the Utopians’ legal principles. The priests have the unique power to declare a disobedient citizen “impious”, which is synonymous with and subject to the same punishments as breaking any other laws of Utopian society.

In Utopia, religion is an institution with a broad definition for a specific purpose – self-sacrifice for the greater good of society. It is a call and motivation to advance society’s benefits. An excellent example of this is when Raphael relates the priests’ visits to the terminally ill to assure the afflicted that there is great honor in easing society’s burdens through auto-termination (83). As Raphael relates Utopia’s structure, it becomes clear that the society’s stability hinges on its ability to adapt to what is best, demonstrated when the author says the citizens of Utopia welcome people from other lands with new skills or ideas (82); the clearest example being when Raphael is persuaded to educate the Utopians in Greek thought and finds them eager students (80). The religious motivation toward improving society at the sacrifice of personal beliefs makes the people of Utopia difficult to assign a single set ideology, but More captures this spirit with the founder, Utopos’ beliefs, saying, “…he made a law, by which everyone was free to practice whatever religion he liked, and to try and convert other people to his own faith, provided he did it quietly and politely, by rational argument (100).” Raphael repeatedly asserts the property-less nature of Utopian society frees each person from the worries common to other societies; religion as More imagines it is a force designed to unite the citizens of Utopia.

Utopian religion is designed and intended to encourage the citizens of Utopia to rationality. More justifies the existence of Utopia and its laws by saying they’re the basis of the happiest and most civilized society (112). It follows from that that in its purpose, Utopia intends religion to proceed from a variety of sources, drawing from the worship of many different things, directing worship toward a single Divine Being. Though there are some basic principles Raphael says are elemental, the Divine Being must and can be discovered only through rational thought and discussion, which means its highest virtue is rationality. The Utopian belief system has to be characterized by this search for what makes the most sense to implement. The author’s assumption is that the most rational way of life would be according to the morals detailed by Raphael, since the Utopian beliefs are presented as a perfected system. However, Raphael tells More that there are some Utopians who reject Utopian society’s values because of their rejection of its most basic religious values (101). It is critical that Utopians believe in the immortality of the soul and in a controlling force in the universe, because these are the principles from which Utopian rationality is derived. Without this common ground, the Utopians who don’t share the core beliefs are liable to have less respect for Utopian rules and authority.

More constructs the religious ideas that guide Utopia from a base of rationality and uses them as a way to motivate its citizens in areas no human solution would suffice. This is apparent in the prayer reported in Utopian churches, where the supplicant begs the Divine Being to be shown where they are wrong, if only to adapt their moral compass to a method more beneficial for society (109). Just as in the argument between Raphael, the Cardinal, and the Lawyer where Raphael espouses the benefit of treating the social cause of theft as opposed to the act, Utopia’s religion is imagined as a way to preemptively prevent social problems by ensuring all Utopians hold the same values as important. When the author speaks of the splendor of Utopian churches, he speaks of a place designed to evoke reverence in congregants, with every element of worship representing a higher purpose (108). More reports of a society where the government rules according to the moral guide of religion, but his intention is to highlight the impossibility of this happening without the state guiding citizens to the ideology that functions best.
