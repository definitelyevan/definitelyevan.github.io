Evan Olsewski - On Christine de Pizan's The Book of the City of Ladies

Pyrrhus’ Perfidious Practice in Penthesilea’s Peril

 

As she constructs a defense of women as human beings worth moral treatment, the angel Reason supplies Christine with many anecdotes detailing the lives of women who defy the negative stereotype the book seeks to dispel. Though the stories might be perceived as an affront to conventional values by men, the author implements them carefully so that each simultaneously disproves any condescending notions of women and calls people of both sexes to a higher standard of moral living. One story in particular, the story of Penthesilea, Queen of the Amazons, is particularly apt at describing the oppressive social situation the author seeks to remedy. Through the situations presented to the characters of Penthesilea’s story, the author allows the characters Reason and Christine to abstractly discuss the misogynist behaviors Reason identifies.

In book one, chapter eight, Reason says that men and women naturally have a strong bond that God created between them, so it is not natural for the sexes to be at odds with one another (Book 1, Chapter 8). Though Reason says, “it’s undeniable that women are by nature fearful creatures, having weak, frail bodies and lacking in physical strength,” and “all women are not as physically strong and courageous as men generally are,” she does not mean that there is an inherent inferiority to womankind, and declares that God created the sexes for equal but different purposes. (Book 1, Chapter 14). In fact, to Reason’s mind, “neither God nor Nature has done the female sex a disservice,” by depriving it of the attributes of bravery and strength since so much evil is able to be perpetrated by them (1, 14). Still, some women are actually gifted with these attributes, and Reason tells the story of Penthesilea as one of the most powerful examples of women using their gifts properly. 

Even in light of these truths, some men still insist upon slandering women, however, Reason says this is not because of a natural disease between the sexes, but because of various flaws in men’s characters. As men are aware of these flaws in themselves, Reason first warns against believing the claims of writers who slander “all women in order to persuade men to regard the entire sex as an abomination,” because they recognize their own shortcomings and wish to shift responsibility for them onto someone else (Book 1, Chapter 8). She similarly holds distaste for men who inflate their egos by simply mimicking the works of superior authors, reproaching both these and the above writers for distributing moral opinions when it is “they themselves, whose habits leave much to be desired” (1, 8). Likewise, the angel notes there are some men who speak ill of women out of envy, either noticing some deficiency in themselves, or fearing a particular woman’s superior skills. Quite accustomed to the pattern of “dissolute behavior” they once enjoyed, men incapacitated by age or injury can only find respite by stealing the enjoyment of these things from those who remain able. Sadly, it is often through demeaning attacks on womankind, making femininity the subject of “vile and disgusting” commentary that these men seek to rest their unmet desires.

In the story of Penthesilea, Reason demonstrates what the various flaws of men look like in the form of Pyrrhus and his men, contrasted to the noble example of the Queen and her ladies. In the beginning, Penthesilea hears tell of the Trojan hero Hector and develops a love for him “with a pure and noble heart,” by the nature of their shared qualities of virtue and strength (Book 1, Chapter 19). As Troy was at war with Greece, Penthesilea resolved to meet and join her noble love in battle. Upon arrival, she finds that Hector has been slain by the Greek warrior Achilles. Grieved by this turn of affairs, Penthesilea takes upon herself the obligation of avenging Hector, emblematic of the “strong and close bond” (Book 1, Chapter 8) the angel Reason praises, embracing the complementary nature of the sexes to which the author refers when describing their natural abilities in Chapter 14. During her pursuit of vengeance, Penthesilea bests and injures Achilles’ son, Pyrrhus, who is rescued from battle on the verge of death. Reason reports that the Greeks “were distraught, for he [Pyrrhus] had been their greatest hope” (Book 1, Chapter 19). The text says that Pyrrhus himself was “overcome with shame and sorrow,” characteristic of Reason’s description of men wont to use gender as a way to demean a superior individual’s skill (Book 1, Chapters 8, 19).

In the latter half of Penthesilea’s story, Pyrrhus demonstrates for the reader the ways which men commonly attack and defeat women, misusing their natural gifts to violate the bonds uniting the sexes. Pyrrhus orders his men to single out Penthesilea so he can “kill her with his own hands” (Book 1, Chapter 19). This is a corrupt directive because Pyrrhus’ declaration after his injury that only he could have Penthesilea matches Reason’s description of the injured slanderer depriving others of his joy described in chapter eight. The charge is also heavy with the understanding that Pyrrhus is acting in a manner contradictory to the natural order of men using strength for justice; he is abusing his gifts (Book 1, Chapter 14). Though Pyrrhus’ men were afraid of Penthesilea, together they managed to surround and disarm her. The story alludes to Reason’s speech in chapter nine, where she describes a united effort by men to keep women isolated and unable to defend themselves from accusations written in the book On the Secrets of Women. She says the group, through the church authority, “excommunicated any man found either reading the book out loud to a woman or giving it to her to read for herself,” since it would take only the testimony of one woman to disprove the “utter rubbish” the book contained, debunking the false claims espoused as true (Book 1, Chapter 9).

At the end of the story, Penthesilea’s death at the hands of Pyrrhus is described in detail intended to paint a portrait of the author’s purpose. It is Pyrrhus’ shame and envy of the Queen’s skill and character that drives him to strike her with such force “that he split her whole skull in two” (Book 1, Chapter 19). Though Penthesilea’s story serves Reason’s end of bearing a good example of noble female conduct, it serves also to establish Christine the author’s claim that such a book defending womankind is necessary. She sought to address and abate the envy-motivated power men exert over women by examining the reasons and ways men felt threatened by those created as their complementary partners. The story of Penthesilea is certainly not one of a physical triumph of women over men, though it is a rebuke of men, an example of women using stereotypically masculine powers in a more fitting way. More than anything else, it is the author’s relation of a story demonstrating the problematic aspects of a ubiquitous worldview. Though she refutes the idea of male superiority, Christine does not attempt to elevate women to a status above men, but through the story illustrates Reason’s words, “It is he or she who is most virtuous who is the superior being,” where men and women lie equal before God, judged only “by the degree to which one has perfected one’s nature and morals” (Book 1, Chapter 9).
