Thesis Topic 1

 

The thesis paper I am interested in writing would investigate the moral laws we have adopted as norms, specifically laws derived from religious texts, and examine the reasoning behind them, how they deviate from the intent of the texts, and how their influence might be positive or negative. I’d like to investigate how the influence of religion has become so foundational to policy in a setting that ideally accommodates all beliefs. Surely the effects of our Abrahamic moral system are still present, but how do we reconcile the evolution of modern society with the mores that are so engrained?

 

The investigation of our foundational beliefs is absolutely critical to our comprehension of current events and new situations. I intend to investigate the moral sources for our society’s mores and values. I believe many instances of friction in society originate from persons holding beliefs that are inconsistent with the greater, perhaps-subconscious worldview shared by a society and making attempts to reconcile those differences through political outlets – that the engine of policy is lenience toward one’s own deviant views while keeping the deviance of others to a tolerable minimum. By studying the underlying values of our moral law, we can see where exactly the unspoken lines between deviance and compliance lie and begin to develop an understanding of cultural movement relative to those lines.

 

In all sciences, one must understand the most basic elements of a system in order to interpret the significance of events as a whole. The science of government is no different. By studying Political Science, I hope to gain an understanding of systems of power, from where that power is drawn, and how that power is applied, not simply effectively but toward what effect. I believe an examination of our past situations cannot harm our understanding of current ones, however it can inform our application of new theories and methods of governance.

 

To perform research for a thesis on this topic would be more than to prepare a paper. It would mean opening myself to accepting findings that do not agree with what I already believe. It would mean using all the resources available to me to survey the broadest possible spectrum of moral inclinations and employ historical evidence as the standard by which our current actions are judged. In our current society, much of our beliefs and systems are inherited from Christian Europe, a setting that interpreted morality in terms of its benefit for the elite and the immovability of divine will in the lot of the common. Whether the European application of morality and its descendant in America differ from the ideal version present in their holy texts is not a question; the information I would explore would answer the question why they do differ, and how that difference has shaped a dissonant exercise of principle. By developing this research, I hope to gain personal perspective on our system, but more than that I wish to point out areas where the gap between belief and application has allowed law to become a tool of power rather than justice.

 

Preparing to write this thesis would not involve drawing on my existing knowledge so much as it would involve practicing diligence in performing the prerequisites. The importance of this study is not in the specific declaration of purpose I make now, before I have learned and gathered information, but in the will behind it. The conflict between beliefs has always existed and I believe always will, however the conflict between a single belief system and its interpretation in policy, for all its opportunity to affect the lives of people, remains imminently malleable for better or worse. By studying and understanding these relationships I believe we can prepare ourselves to have a meaningful impact on those around us; by examining the cause of moral friction we can guide our own conduct in anticipation of consequence.
