Evan Olszewski

The Missional Nature of “The Dream of the Rood”

 

Conceived to spread the news of salvation in the most succinct and effective way possible, epistolic works have for centuries met the reader halfway between the world with which they are familiar and that with which the author seeks to familiarise them. Often, authors synthesise cultural stories or songs people already know with their gospel so the adaptation appeals more easily than the alien story. In the critical essay, Adelheid Theime’s “Gift Giving as a Vital Element of Salvation in ‘The Dream of the Rood’”, the author discusses the role of Germanic cultural rituals used metaphorically or illustratively in the poem “The Dream of the Rood” to inform a pagan audience about the role of Christ in our lives.  “Dream”’s descent from and obvious inspiration by oral poetry of the region means that the poet intentionally borrowed cultural conventions the audience would recognize in order to effectively disseminate the message of the Cross.

 

Theime’s essay discusses one particular Germanic tradition, the transactional exchange of gifts between lord and vassal, and makes the case that the relationship between the Rood and Christ in the poem follows that same scheme; essentially, “Dream” is a Christian message reskinned to fit within the Germanic cultural expectations of a hero and his deeds. The “Dream” poet deliberately likens the relationship between the Rood, the subject and protagonist of the poem, and Christ to the familiar relationship between vassals and regional lords in order to create an accessibility of gospel for the common listener. The poet characterizes Christ as a prototypical epic hero to which the Rood must pay obeisance when he is referred to as a “young hero”, a common way to refer to warriors in Germanic tradition, during his introduction (Dream line 39). Theime picks up on this, noting that the poet stoops to adapt the significance of Christ’s sacrifice in a distinctly Anglo-Saxon context (Theime, pg. 112). Arguing the relationship between Christ and the Rood is one governed by the “principle of reciprocity”, one the common people would see frequently practiced in their dealings with nobles and lords, Theime highlights the poet’s skillful relation of that same principle as the ideal for the readers’ relationship to Christ, modelled by the Rood itself (109). The essayist insists the character of the Rood and the role it models are not functions of stylistic liberty, but rather were engineered to impact the target audience in the most relevant and familiar manner.

 

I believe the Rood as portrayed by the “Dream” poet is a model for how we should see ourselves. Theime says that “the Rood poet fights a sense of alienation from the new ideas and expectations of Christianity” in its largely-pagan audience by relating its message to the cultural background with which they were already familiar, striving to create something to which the audience could relate while still maintaining the spirit of the original message (109). This is an interesting implication because it means we can examine the rest of “Dream” with an eye for cultural rephrasing of critical elements of the story and understand their relevance both to the gospel message and to the poet themselves. One of the most important sections of “Dream”, the declaration of the risen Christ’s power, lends us a glimpse into the integrity of the poet in his adaptation:

 

“Nor need there then any be most afraid 
who ere in his breast bears finest of beacons;
but through that rood shall each soul
from the earth-way enter the kingdom,
who with the Wielder thinks yet to dwell.” (Dream lines 117-121)

 

By examining this passage with Theime’s reckoning, I believe this is exactly where his case is most strongly made. In this passage, the tendency to rely on merit to get gifts from a ruler is both upheld and subverted in that it is no longer about the signs, the “finest of beacons” on man’s outside, but through internal obedience to and acceptance of the gospel message “through that rood” that salvation is bestowed (118-119). We also see the references to God and the Earth in contemporarily Germanic ways, alluding to a compatibility of existing beliefs with the revelatory truth unfolded in the poem (120-121). In “Dream”, salvation for the Rood and in an allusion, the readers or mankind, is the gift given in exchange for obedience. I absolutely agree with Theime’s theory that the Rood’s relationship with Christ illustrates our relationship with God in a way adapted to the cultural perspective of the poet’s audience, one preoccupied with a merit-based system of exchange, and makes the message fit within the logic of their existing social structure.

 

In reality, many of our modern religious works are likely constructed in a similar manner to the “Dream” poet’s work, but without the separation of centuries and culture it is difficult to detect where and how we might adapt our perspectives to our culture. The neatly-packaged manner in which cultural fixtures are blended into a religious work created in me a need to go back and examine other works both from this same era and others to see what I might have missed. The outward-reaching nature of epistolic works requires an art so finely balanced between accommodation and compromise often critical details are in danger of being lost to nuance. Theime’s writing acknowledges the missional intent of “Dream” and neatly calls out points of recognition that would otherwise remain unnoticeable. Because cultures tend to bleed into one another unintentionally over time, it can be difficult to catch the purposeful introduction of one to another, but Theime does exactly that, introducing the reader to a creative cultural bridge and its potent meaning to the original audience.
