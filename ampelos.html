Ampelos

Evan Olszewski

 

Allow me to tell of my time as Ampelos, the grape-gatherer, beloved of Bacchus. As the legend goes, the god Bacchus entrusted a young satyr with a grapevine growing among the leaves of a colossal elm. The youth tended the grapes in the tree and kept them nourished, always dreaming of grander fruits, but met his death falling from the highest branch, reaching for a bunch more excellent than all the rest. Though the moral value of the story may be related easily to many of our lives, I bring it up for the literal parallel to my situation and for the symbolism behind it. The fruit for which we reach and the reward our efforts deserve are often quite different.

For several summers now I have been fortunate enough to intern at a small vineyard in Yankton, South Dakota, a place that is as alone as it can possibly be without actually feeling lonely. Bacchus, in this story, is my Uncle Mark and Aunt Kim, the owners of the place and sole keepers of the property. Over the course of years, they have taught me the basics of tending vines and watching weather, when to water and when to cover, when to gather or when to simply let the vines be. Besides the field work, I have also been taught the many processes contributing to the production of wine. In my story, Bacchus, content with the knowledge Ampelos had gathered gave him a little space to explore, to find his own method of doing things, perhaps even on his own time.

For a few weeks at the beginning of the summer Ampelos held back, afraid to commit to one strategy, one path. He bothered Bacchus at every turn, asking the best way to do this, the most effe ctive way to do that, but Bacchus never minded. Kindly, each process would be re-explained; Bacchus would talk of how the vines were planted, why they were groomed as they were, how the birds could be persuaded not to come, how to check the wholesomeness of each fruit, but as Ampelos listened more and more he heard ever less and less. The starling fruit he could see in his mind’s eye grew from a vine shaped more by luck and skill than of the dry roots which wrapped things like preparation and procedure. Bacchus only smiled and continued to tend the vines the same way they always had been, even as the youth experimented, flitting from idea to idea like the birds in the bunches. Thus a whole season was whiled away.

I fell from the elm only a couple weeks before harvest time. Like the satyr in the old story, I neglected to consider the banal steps of preparation throughout the season and instead extended my hand much further than I should have dared. For a second, perhaps, my fingertips grazed the skins of the grapes I’d imagined, but the balance was lost. The vines I cared for appeared sickly, the land under them fallow, while the ones tended by my aunt and uncle were full of life, courted incessantly by insects and birds the same. Not even the animals were tempted by my fruit. The only grapes I and the satyr possessed were ones we imagined from a distance too great to bridge with our intentions. The death of Ampelos was tragic, not in a typical mourning sense, but in the way that resonates with a person’s innate desire for success. One job was all he had, but it was his determination to perform it perfectly that killed him - he was so taken by the possibilities, the realities were forgotten. You might think the message of the legend is to discourage greed or to encourage people to find contentment; I, Ampelos, can earnestly tell you, however, that the lesson learned is the difference between the fruit we imagine and the work we implement.

All people can recall a certain moment where it became clear to them that the fruit they would gather depended on the preparation they did beforehand. That which you sow, you will also reap, as the saying goes. An important part of functioning as a human being is forecasting the consequences of our actions and altering the things we do to affect the results. While I cannot pretend that my sense of this has developed any more keenly than any others’, the extraordinary opportunity of seeing the parable unfold in my own life has made me acutely aware of the importance of a solid foundation in life. The groundwork I am doing for my career now has been carefully considered and formulated. The work I do and the opportunities I seize now are the most important to me because it is diligence of the moment that will yield the fruit for which I reach.
